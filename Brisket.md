# BBQ Brisket (6–8kg Whole Packer)

**Best cooker:** Kamado with heat deflector — low and slow is exactly what brisket needs.  
The Weber kettle can work in a pinch but requires more babysitting to hold temperature.

---

## The Cut

A 6–8kg whole packer brisket includes both the **flat** and the **point**, untrimmed.

This is what you want for American-style BBQ. The point has the fat and collagen that make brisket worth cooking properly. Ask your butcher specifically for the whole brisket, untrimmed — don't accept just the flat. If they look confused, try Turner & George or The Dorset Meat Company online.

Trim the fat cap down to around **6–8mm** — enough to baste the meat through the cook, not so much that it never renders down.

---

## Rub

| Ingredient          | Amount |
|---------------------|--------|
| Coarse sea salt     | 50g    |
| Coarse black pepper | 35g    |
| Garlic powder       | 15g    |
| Smoked paprika      | 15g    |
| Mustard powder      | 15g    |

Mix together. Apply the night before — this isn't optional at this size.
Coat all surfaces thoroughly. The fat cap gets it too.

---

## Setup — Kamado

- **Target temperature:** 110–120°C (230–250°F) at grate level
- **Heat deflector:** in place — indirect heat throughout
- **Wood:** 4–5 chunks of oak or cherry (not chips — chunks last longer). Add to coals before the brisket goes on.
- **Water pan:** worth using at this size. Helps stabilise humidity over a very long cook. A foil tray works fine.
- **Charcoal:** fill the kamado fully — you'll need it for an 18–22 hour cook. Lump charcoal only.

Light a small amount of lump charcoal, let the kamado come up to temp slowly.
Vent adjustment is everything on a kamado — small changes, be patient.

---

## Cook

| Stage         | Details                                                              |
|---------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Orientation   | Fat cap down, facing the deflector plate                             |
| Duration      | 18–22 hours at 115°C — go by temperature, not time                  |
| Bark formation | Around 70–75°C internal, the stall begins                          |
| The stall     | Internal temp plateaus for 2–4 hours at this size. Normal. Leave it alone. |
| Wrap          | At 75°C internal — wrap tightly in butcher's paper, then a layer of foil over the top |
| Pull temp     | **93–96°C** internal                                                 |
| Probe feel    | Should go in like warm butter with no resistance across both flat and point |

Use a decent dual-probe thermometer — one in the flat, one in the point. The flat will finish first. Don't pull until both probe correctly.

---

## The Stall — What It Is

Evaporative cooling stalls the internal temperature, sometimes for hours.
At this size expect it to be stubborn. Wrapping in butcher's paper pushes through it while keeping some bark integrity.

Foil works but softens the bark more. Best of both: a layer of butcher's paper then foil over the outside.

---

## Rest

**Non-negotiable, and longer than you think at this size.** Rest the wrapped brisket for a minimum of **2 hours**, ideally 3–4. Wrap in towels and put it in a cool box — it'll stay hot for 4–5 hours easily and will only improve.

Do not skip the rest. Slicing too early is how you ruin it.

---

## Slice

- Separate the point from the flat along the fat seam before slicing
- Slice the **flat** against the grain, around 8–10mm thick
- The **point** can be cubed for burnt ends, or sliced against its own grain (it runs at an angle to the flat)
- Serve immediately

---

## Rough Timeline (7kg, 115°C)

| Time         | Event                                      |
|--------------|--------------------------------------------|
| Night before | Apply rub, refrigerate                     |
| 05:00        | Light kamado, stabilise temp               |
| 06:00        | Brisket on                                 |
| ~16:00       | Wrap at 75°C internal (expect the stall to drag) |
| ~02:00       | Pull at 93–96°C                            |
| ~05:00       | Rest complete, slice and serve             |

This is an overnight cook. Plan accordingly. A good probe thermometer with an alarm takes most of the anxiety out of it.

---

## Notes

- Don't open the lid constantly. Every peek drops temperature and adds time — even more critical over a 20-hour cook.
- Lump charcoal only on a kamado for cooks this long — burns cleaner and longer than briquettes.
- If the bark isn't properly set when you hit 75°C, wait until it is before wrapping.
- Check charcoal levels around the halfway mark if you're awake. A fully loaded kamado should make it, but worth confirming.
- Leftovers reheat well wrapped in foil with a splash of beef stock at 150°C for 30–40 minutes. A brisket this size will have plenty.
