# BBQ Brisket (2kg Boneless)

**Best cooker:** Kamado with heat deflector — low and slow is exactly what brisket needs.  
The Weber kettle can work in a pinch but requires more babysitting to hold temperature.

---

## The Cut

A 2kg boneless brisket is likely a **flat** or a rolled brisket joint from a British butcher.

If you can get it, ask for the **point** end or a rolled **whole brisket** — more fat marbling, better result. Supermarket "brisket joint" is usually fine but lean side; the fat cap is your friend, don't trim it aggressively.

---

## Rub

| Ingredient         | Amount  |
|--------------------|---------|
| Coarse sea salt    | 15g     |
| Coarse black pepper | 10g    |
| Garlic powder      | 5g      |
| Smoked paprika     | 5g      |
| Mustard powder     | 5g      |

Mix together. Apply the night before or at minimum 2 hours before cooking.
Coat all surfaces — don't be shy. The fat cap gets it too.

---

## Setup — Kamado

- **Target temperature:** 110–120°C (230–250°F) at grate level
- **Heat deflector:** in place — you want indirect heat throughout
- **Wood:** 2–3 chunks of oak or cherry (not chips — chunks last longer).
  Soak is optional, most people skip it. Add to coals before the brisket goes on.
- **Water pan:** optional but helps stabilise humidity. A small foil tray works.

Light a small amount of lump charcoal, let the kamado come up to temp slowly.
Vent adjustment is everything on a kamado — small changes, be patient.

---

## Cook

| Stage              | Details                                              |
|--------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| Fat cap down       | Start fat cap facing the deflector plate             |
| Duration           | Roughly 6–8 hours for 2kg, but go by temperature    |
| Bark formation     | Around 70–75°C internal, the stall begins            |
| The stall          | Internal temp plateaus for 1–3 hours. Normal. Leave it alone. |
| Wrap               | At 75°C internal — wrap tightly in butcher's paper (preferred) or foil |
| Pull temp          | **93–96°C** internal                                 |
| Probe feel         | Should go in like warm butter with no resistance     |

Use a decent probe thermometer. Don't rely on time alone.

---

## The Stall — What It Is

Evaporative cooling stalls the internal temperature, sometimes for hours.
Wrapping in butcher's paper pushes through it while keeping some bark integrity.

Foil works but softens the bark more. Your call.

I wrap a layer of butchers paper then put a layer of foil on the outside.

---

## Rest

**This is non-negotiable.** Rest the wrapped brisket for a minimum of **1 hour**,
ideally 2. Wrap in a towel and put it in a cool box if you need to hold it longer —
it'll stay hot for 3–4 hours easily.

Do not skip the rest. The juices redistribute. Slicing too early is how you ruin it.

---

## Slice

- Slice **against the grain** — important with brisket flat
- Around 8–10mm thick
- Serve immediately

---

## Rough Timeline (2kg, 115°C)

| Time       | Event                        |
|------------|------------------------------|
| Night before | Apply rub, refrigerate     |
| 07:00      | Light kamado, stabilise temp |
| 08:00      | Brisket on                   |
| ~14:00     | Wrap at 75°C internal        |
| ~16:00     | Pull at 93–96°C              |
| ~17:00     | Rest complete, slice and serve |

Times are approximate — the brisket is done when it probes correctly, not on schedule.

---

## Notes

- Don't open the lid constantly. Every peek drops temperature and adds time.
- Lump charcoal is better than briquettes on a kamado for long cooks — burns cleaner.
- If the bark isn't set when you hit 75°C, wait until it is before wrapping.
- Leftovers reheat well wrapped in foil with a splash of beef stock at 150°C for 30 minutes.
