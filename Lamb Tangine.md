Lamb Tagine — Hob Sear, Kamado Braise
A Moroccan-spiced lamb tagine — seared on the hob while the kamado comes up to temperature, then finished low and slow over indirect heat with a hint of fruit wood smoke.

INGREDIENTS
• 1.1 kilograms lamb shoulder, cut into 5cm chunks
• 2 tablespoons olive oil
• 2 tablespoons argan oil (or extra olive oil)
• 2 large onions, grated
• 3 garlic cloves, crushed
• 570 milliliters tomato juice
• 2 tins chopped tomatoes (400g each)
• 600 milliliters lamb stock
• 115 grams dried apricots, halved
• 55 grams dates, halved
• 55 grams sultanas or raisins
• 85 grams flaked almonds
• 1 tablespoons clear honey
• 1 teaspoons saffron, soaked in cold water
• 1 teaspoons cayenne pepper
• 2 teaspoons ground black pepper
• 1.5 tablespoons paprika
• 1.5 tablespoons ground ginger
• 1 tablespoons turmeric
• 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon
• 2 tablespoons fresh coriander, roughly chopped
• 2 tablespoons fresh flatleaf parsley, roughly chopped

STEPS
1. Light the kamado: Light the kamado and set it up for indirect cooking with deflector plates in place. Target temperature is 160–170°C. Add a small chunk of apple or cherry wood for smoke. Leave it to come up to temperature while you work on the hob.
2. Mix the spice blend: Combine 1 teaspoons cayenne pepper, 2 teaspoons ground black pepper, 1.5 tablespoons paprika, 1.5 tablespoons ground ginger, 1 tablespoons turmeric, and 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon in a small bowl. Toss the 1.1 kilograms lamb shoulder, cut into 5cm chunks in the spice mix until evenly coated.
3. Sear the lamb on the hob: Heat 2 tablespoons olive oil and 2 tablespoons argan oil (or extra olive oil) in the Dutch oven over a high heat on the hob until shimmering. Sear the lamb in batches until well browned on all sides — don't crowd the pot or it'll steam rather than colour. Set the seared meat aside.
4. Soften onions and garlic: Reduce to a medium heat. Cook the 2 large onions, grated in the same pot until softened and starting to colour, scraping up any browned bits from the bottom. Add 3 garlic cloves, crushed and cook for another minute or two.
5. Build the braise: Return the lamb to the pot. Add 570 milliliters tomato juice, 2 tins chopped tomatoes (400g each), 600 milliliters lamb stock, 115 grams dried apricots, halved, 55 grams dates, halved, 55 grams sultanas or raisins, 1 tablespoons clear honey, and 1 teaspoons saffron, soaked in cold water. Stir to combine — the liquid should nearly cover the meat. Bring to a gentle simmer on the hob.
6. Transfer to the kamado: Check the kamado is holding steady at 160–170°C. Transfer the Dutch oven onto the kamado grate, lid on. Close the kamado lid and leave it alone — every peek costs temperature. Braise for 2–2.5 hours.
7. Check at one hour: After an hour, quickly check the liquid level and make sure nothing is catching. Top up with a splash of stock if needed, then close up again.
8. Toast the almonds: In a dry frying pan, toast the 85 grams flaked almonds until golden. Keep an eye on them — they catch quickly.
9. Check for tenderness: The tagine is done when the lamb is completely tender and falling apart. Check with a fork rather than the clock — it may need anywhere from 2 to 2.5 hours.
10. Finish and serve: Scatter the toasted 85 grams flaked almonds over the top and finish with 2 tablespoons fresh coriander, roughly chopped and 2 tablespoons fresh flatleaf parsley, roughly chopped. Serve with couscous or flatbreads.

NOTES
**Timing the kamado:** Light it before you start cooking. By the time you've done the sear, softened the onions, and built the braise, it should be approaching 160–170°C. Close the vents down early — it's easier to hold temperature than to chase it down from too high.

**Fruit wood:** A small chunk of apple or cherry works well with the Moroccan spices. Don't overdo it — you want a hint of smoke, not a bonfire.

**On the sear:** Get the Dutch oven properly hot before the meat goes in. Work in batches and don't move the meat until it releases naturally.

**On the argan oil:** Worth tracking down — it adds a nutty depth that olive oil won't replicate. Most good delis or Middle Eastern grocers stock it.

**On the liquid:** Plenty of moisture between the tomato juice and stock. The Dutch oven lid traps steam well, so you're unlikely to need much topping up.
