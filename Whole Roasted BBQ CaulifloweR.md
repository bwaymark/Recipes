# Whole Roasted BBQ Cauliflower (Kamado)

A smoky, whole-head cauliflower cooked low and slow on the kamado, finished with a buttery spice baste for a tender interior and proper charred bark.

**Serves:** 4 **Total time:** ~2–2.5 hours (plus 45 min–1 hr brine)

## Ingredients

- 1 large cauliflower head
- 60g salt (for brine)
- 1l water (for brine)
- 60g unsalted butter, melted
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- 2 tsp smoked paprika
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- ½ tsp cayenne pepper
- ½ tsp black pepper

## Method

1. **Brine the cauliflower** — Dissolve the salt in the water. Trim the outer leaves but keep the core intact, then submerge fully (core down, weighted with a plate if needed). Leave at room temperature for 45 minutes to 1 hour.
    
2. **Dry the cauliflower** — Remove from the brine and pat thoroughly dry, including between the florets. Any leftover moisture steams rather than chars, so don't rush this.
    
3. **Mix the baste** — Combine the melted butter, olive oil, smoked paprika, cumin, garlic powder, cayenne and black pepper in a small bowl.
    
4. **Set up the kamado** — Get it stable at 150°C (300°F) with the heat deflector in for indirect cooking. 

5. **First baste and cook** — Brush the cauliflower all over with about a third of the butter mixture. Place over indirect heat, lid down, for 1 hour.
    
6. **Baste again** — Open the lid, brush over another third of the mixture (get into the gaps between florets), close the lid, continue cooking.
    
7. **Finish cooking** — Cook for a further 45 minutes or so, until a knife slides into the centre with no resistance. Baste once more partway through with the remaining mixture.
    
8. **Rest and serve** — Lift off and rest 5–10 minutes before cutting into wedges. Good with chimichurri, tahini-lemon, or a sharp yoghurt sauce.
    

## Notes

Total cook time is roughly 2–2.5 hours depending on the size of the head and how steady you keep the kamado at 150°C. For more bark, open the deflector for the last 10 minutes to expose it to direct heat — watch it closely, as it'll catch quickly once exposed to flame.
